Linear Regression Cost Explorer

What this tool does:
  • Build a practice dataset: pick the slope and intercept of a hidden straight line, add some random scatter, and the tool plots the resulting data points for you.
  • See the single straight line that fits those points best, found with the standard least-squares method, plus an R² score saying how closely the line matches the data (1 is a perfect fit).
  • Get a feel for "cost", one number measuring how far a line sits from the data, so a lower cost means a better line. The tool shows the cost of every possible line as a colour map and as a rotatable 3D landscape.
  • Try your own lines by typing in a slope and intercept, and see that line drawn on the data together with its cost and R².
  • Watch gradient descent, the step-by-step "walk downhill" method computers use, start from a guess and edge toward the best line one small step at a time.
  • Follow a chart of the cost after each step to watch the method settle onto the answer, or shoot off to infinity when the steps are made too big.

How it works

A line is written y = m x + b, where m is the slope and b is the intercept. For each data point the "error" is the vertical gap between the line and the point. The cost squares every error and averages them, so one big miss counts a lot:

J(m, b) = (1 / 2n) · ∑ (m·x + b − y)²

Gradient descent starts from a guess and repeatedly nudges m and b a little in the direction that lowers the cost. How big each nudge is depends on the learning rate α:

m ← m − α · ∂J/∂m
b ← b − α · ∂J/∂b

A small α learns slowly but safely; too large an α overshoots and the cost blows up. Because this cost valley is long and narrow, the path naturally bends rather than heading straight to the bottom.
